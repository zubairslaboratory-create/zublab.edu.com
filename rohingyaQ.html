About the Hanifi Rohingya Script

The Hanifi Rohingya script was developed in the 1980s by the Rohingya Language Committee under the guidance of Maulana Mohammed Hanif, giving the script its name "Hanifi". This script was created to provide the Rohingya people with a unique writing system that reflects their own culture and history.

The script has an Arabic flavor, reflecting the Rohingya religion and history, as they trace their descent to Arab traders and other groups who have been in the region for generations. It is written right-to-left and is considered one of the most attractive invented scripts for a living language in recent times.

Today, the script is taught in Rohingya community schools in refugee camps, with an estimated 25,000 children learning it in Bangladesh alone.

About the Script & Font

Historical Development

The Hanifi Rohingya script was developed in the 1980s to create a unique writing system for the Rohingya language. Before this, Rohingya had been written in Latin, Arabic, Urdu and Burmese scripts [citation:2].

Digital Fonts

Muhammad Noor developed the first digital fonts for Hanifi Rohingya in the early 2000s, creating two typefaces known as "Noories kuna leka" and "Noories Guwaiiyya leka" [citation:6]. The font you mentioned, 'Arif Noor Muzhary', appears to be part of this font development tradition.

Language Preservation

Despite persecution and displacement, the Rohingya community continues to teach their language and script. There are about 50 Rohingya community schools in Bangladesh refugee camps teaching the Hanifi script [citation:2].

Unicode Standard

The Hanifi Rohingya script was added to the Unicode Standard in 2018 (version 11.0) in the range U+10D00‚ÄìU+10D3F. This allows it to be used digitally across different platforms and devices [citation:6].
